# Assessment Design for L&D Programs

## Purpose of Assessments

Assessments serve two functions: they verify that learning happened (summative), and they reinforce learning through retrieval practice (formative). Both matter. A program with no assessments can't prove value. A program with only a final exam misses the learning benefit of frequent low-stakes testing.

## Assessment Types

### Knowledge Checks (Formative)
Short quizzes embedded throughout a course. 3-5 questions after each module. Purpose is reinforcement, not gatekeeping. Should be low-stakes (not graded or allowing retakes). Research shows retrieval practice improves long-term retention by 20-40% compared to re-reading.

Best practices:
- Use scenario-based questions, not recall ("What would you do if..." not "What year was...")
- Provide immediate feedback with explanations
- Space them throughout the content, not bunched at the end
- Allow retakes — the point is learning, not filtering

### Skill Assessments (Summative)
Verify that the learner can perform the target skill. These should map directly to the learning objectives. If the objective is "configure a firewall rule," the assessment should require configuring a firewall rule — not answering multiple choice questions about firewalls.

Types by fidelity:
- **Multiple choice**: Tests recognition, not production. Useful for knowledge but poor for skills. Easy to author and grade.
- **Scenario-based**: Presents a realistic situation and asks for a decision or action. Better than MCQ for testing judgment. Harder to author.
- **Simulation**: Learner performs the task in a realistic environment. Highest fidelity but expensive to build.
- **Project/portfolio**: Learner produces a work product. Best for complex, creative skills. Requires human evaluation.
- **Peer assessment**: Learners evaluate each other's work against a rubric. Scales well and builds critical thinking. Requires calibration.

### Certification Exams
High-stakes assessments that gate a credential. Require careful psychometric design.

Best practices:
- Use a question bank (3-5x the exam length) to prevent memorization
- Set pass scores based on a standard-setting study, not arbitrary percentages
- Include a mix of difficulty levels (roughly 30% easy, 50% medium, 20% hard)
- Track item statistics — questions everyone gets right or wrong aren't measuring anything
- Offer retakes with a cooling period (7-14 days) and different questions

## Common Assessment Anti-Patterns

**The trivial quiz**: "Click Next to complete the assessment." This exists only to generate a completion metric. It proves nothing and teaches nothing.

**The gotcha exam**: Obscure questions testing details no one needs in practice. Demoralizes learners and measures memorization, not competency.

**The one-shot final**: All assessment weight on a single end-of-course exam. High anxiety, no formative benefit, poor signal (could be a bad day).

**Assessment-content mismatch**: The course teaches concepts but the assessment tests procedures, or vice versa. Common when assessments are written by different people than the content.

## Mapping Assessments to Bloom's Taxonomy

Use Bloom's levels to match assessment types to learning objectives:

- **Remember/Understand**: Multiple choice, matching, true/false
- **Apply**: Scenario-based questions, worked examples, simulations
- **Analyze**: Case studies, data interpretation, compare/contrast tasks
- **Evaluate**: Peer review, critique exercises, decision justification
- **Create**: Projects, portfolios, design challenges

Most corporate training lives at the Apply level — that's where behavior change happens. If all your assessments are at Remember/Understand, you're testing the wrong thing.
